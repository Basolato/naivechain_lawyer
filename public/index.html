Welcome to NaiveChain


NaiveChain is a system for lawyers. It enables them to build contracts together, without the hassle to check for differences in new drafts. It easily can be seen, when the counter party is ready to sign the Contract. Therefore, it takes significant less time to draft contracts together.

NaiveChain builds on top of a Blockchain architecture. Blockchain technology is mostly known for its use in cryptocurrencies, but it finds rising usages among other use cases. Blockchain provides you with different key security factors:

  • Integrity: Manipulation of contract information or content is not possible and is detected immediately.
  • Authenticity: The identity of your counter party is ensured via digital signatures.
  • Undeniability: If changes are made, it is not possible to delete them.
  • Confidentiality: The contract you write is kept between you and your counterparty.
  • Trustlessness: You do not have to trust your counterparty. All changes are fully transparent.
This page gives you comprehensible information about the system of NaiveChain.

Document Page

The Document page provides you with information about your current document. In the main area titled with Current Document you see the last status of your document. If you are starting the system for the first time, the only thing that is there will be Vertrag. You cannot change the content in there, but you can add additional lines. Just go unter the view to the view Add additional Lines and write whatever suits you. Once you hit submit, the lines will be added to your contract and to your underlying blockchain. On the right to the main window you see an area labeled with Versions. This provides you with the possibilty to jump between versions and see the changes that are made by whom.

Blocks Page

The blocks page gives you detailed information about your underlying blockchain. In the first window labeled BlockView you get a visual representation of your current blockchain. Under that, you see a full list of all the single blocks in your blockchain. All content is provided, including the index, the nonce, a previous hash, a timestamp, the data, the status, the creatorID, the hash and the signature. You can hover over every to get the content of the corresponding field. Under the list, you are able to add new blocks to the chain. This is not advisable, because it could break your current document.

Forking in Blockchain

    • Settings 1
    • Settings 2

Forking is a process that happens regularly in blockchain technology. Normally, this happens randomly without any intention to do so. We divide forking into three different categories: random forking, soft forking and hard forking.

Random Forking

Some Blockchain systems (mostly public cryptocrurrencies) try to publish all x minutes a new block in average. In Bitcoin, x is 10 Minutes, other coins use other values for x. In average means, that it is possible that a new block is found in under one minute or it could also take up half an hour before a block gets found. With this property, it is also possible that two blocks gets published simultaneously. For a short moment two chains with the same length exist. The chain wins, which finds faster a block. You can try that yourself with the controller under this article.

51% Attacks in Blockchain

    • Settings 1
    • Settings 2

The 51-% attack is one of the better known attack scenarios which blockchains face. Blockchains work like a democracy: the network and all its nodes agree on a certain blockchain to be the “true” one. If the majority lies in the hands of a single entity, this attack scenario becomes possible and it decides on its own, which chain is the good one. What is the attacker capable of? First, he cannot create money out of thin air (except the mining reward) or change any balances, he has to present the network a legitimate chain, which means every transaction has to be valid and comprehensible. This leaves two attack scenarios: a double spending attack and a denial of service. Giving that the attacker has the majority of the network, it could propose a transaction (and include it in the blockchain) for that he receives a good. After he got it, he goes back to a certain block (before the transaction was included) and builds from there a new branch, where the transaction is not included. Given enough time, he will wind up with the longest chain and the network will agree upon that. The denial of service (DoS)-attack will work as follows: Because he has the majority, he doesn’t need to build upon other proposed blocks, because he will always wind up with the longest chain. When that’s the case, he decides which transactions to include in the block and which not. He could therefore “freeze” certain addresses/wallets, so the money becomes unspendable. He can blackmail owners of these wallets or could claim higher transaction fees. A subspecies of this attack is the “hybrid mining”, where small cryptocurrencies are attacked. Most altcoins work similar to the BitCoin-system and therefore it is possible, to propose the same blocks, which are proposed in the bitcoin network, to the blockchain of the new altcoin. Small currencies with less mining power are very vulnerable to this attack, because big mining rigs can take up easily a big part of the network.
